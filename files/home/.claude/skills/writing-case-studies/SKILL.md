# Writing a case study

A case study is a narrative sales asset, not a portfolio piece. Its job is to reduce a future client's perceived risk. The client is the hero — never you.

## Step 1 — Decide who it's for

- **Seeker Type** (what the reader wants to confirm): Payoff (ROI), Aesthetic, Process, Proof, Prestige, or Expertise. Write the body toward the type(s) that match your positioning.
- **Positioning Archetype**: aim for **The Expert** (the only one with no real downside). Avoid The Cheap Date.

## Step 2 — Gather the inputs (Job Jacket)

Collect (or ask the user for): the client's compelling event and how they found you; the business goal (the Mission — never "we made a logo"); the obstacles and process; the decisive choice; the quantified outcome; a testimonial; their exact words. If inputs are thin, ask for the missing pieces before drafting.

## Step 3 — Structure

1. **Title** — pick the pattern that carries the most value: Brand Boast, Outcome/Goal, Brand & Insight, or The Challenge.
2. **Summary** (for the skimmer): Mission (the business goal) → Outcome (what you made) → Impact (quantified change — the most important line) → Services. Hide Awards/Press if none.
3. **Body** — tell the story with the **4 Gs**: **Goal** (the hero and their normal) → **Gap** (obstacles, your process) → **Gamble** (the decisive choice / the risk the client took) → **Gain** (the new normal, the impact). Weight Gamble and Gain heaviest.
4. **Call to action** — never leave a convinced reader nowhere to go (related studies, contact, capabilities deck).

## Step 4 — Avoid the anti-patterns

- "Isn't It Pretty?" (image dump, no story) · pure chronology (no climax) · Design-Snob-Scholar (kerning talk business clients don't care about).
- If the client was difficult, frame their eventual decision as courage, not you winning a fight.
- Prefer real-world photography; include human faces in process shots; standardize hero-image treatment across studies.
