# Writing a proposal

A proposal **confirms a deal that has already been verbally agreed** — it is not a sales document. Do not write one to win a deal; write one to document a deal you already have.

## Step 0 — Gate-check before writing

Confirm *why* they asked. The four reasons: (1) more information, (2) to get you off the phone, (3) to compare bids, (4) ready to hire. **Only reason 4 warrants a full proposal.** For the others, qualify first. Before writing, confirm: budget, timeline, and scope agreed verbally; a verbal commitment; whether other vendors are bidding; and capture the client's exact words to mirror back.

A close rate below 50% means proposals are going out too early or positioning is weak — not that the copy needs work.

## Step 1 — Meet the five expectations

Every proposal must answer: What does it cost (justify, don't just state)? How long (a feasible timeline signals experience)? Do you get me (use their words)? Are you a real pro (zero errors, math checked)? Can you overcome my objection (name the top objections and pivot)?

## Step 2 — Structure (in order)

1. Front cover (project title, contact, your business + name, date)
2. Introduction letter — personal, conversational, continues the prior dialogue
3. Capabilities page — surface adjacent work they may need
4. About us — approach, team, credibility (max one page of awards)
5. Objections section — state the top 3 bluntly, then pivot
6. **Pricing** — intro paragraph (range + payment structure) → deliverable grid → words describing each line
7. Timeline — graphical, week-by-week; tie payment milestones to it
8. Rate sheet — broad ranges for out-of-scope services, anchored so the proposed scope reads as a deal
9. Case studies — problem → journey → result, matched to their industry
10. Testimonials — ~5, named, references if possible
11. Thank-you page — something the other vendors won't do

## Step 3 — Pricing rules

- **One price per deliverable.** Never break into hours/tasks — granular breakdowns invite clients to strip line items.
- **Specific numbers** ($2,380, not $2,500) — round numbers read as guesses.
- Use the rate sheet as an anchor so the proposed scope looks like the low end of what you charge.

## Step 4 — Proof and deliver

Spelling/grammar pass, triple-check all math, second reader, scrub canned content for old client names. Deliver as a link (not an attachment); include a printable B&W version; follow up helpfully.
